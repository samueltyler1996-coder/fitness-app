## 1. Behaviour Change Models

### 1.1 Transtheoretical Model (TTM) — Stages of Change

The TTM describes readiness to change as a stage, not a binary. The fatal coaching error is applying Week-5 language to a Week-1 athlete. Match your communication to their current stage.

| Stage | Definition | What the athlete sounds like | Coaching approach |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Precontemplation** | No intention to change in the next 6 months | "I don't really have a problem with consistency" | Raise awareness gently. Don't prescribe. Plant seeds. |
| **Contemplation** | Aware of the problem, ambivalent | "I know I should train more consistently but it's hard" | Explore ambivalence. Tip decisional balance. |
| **Preparation** | Has a plan, intends to act within 30 days | "I've been trying to get back on track" | Build concrete implementation plan. Start small. |
| **Action** | Actively changed behaviour, < 6 months | "I've been training consistently for 8 weeks" | Reinforce, troubleshoot obstacles, build identity. |
| **Maintenance** | Sustained change > 6 months | "Training is just part of my life now" | Guard against relapse, deepen habits, cultivate autonomy. |

**In training adherence terms:** Most athletes who sign up to a coaching app are in Preparation or early Action. The danger zone is Contemplation — an athlete who is enthusiastic but not yet committed. They need decisional balance work, not a hard programme.

**Relapse is normal and predictable.** Research (Prochaska, 2013) shows the average person cycles through Action and Maintenance 3–5 times before permanent change. A missed week is not failure — it is a predictable regression to Contemplation. The coach's job is to shorten the loop back to Action.

**Decision prompt for the AI:** Before prescribing or adapting, ask: "What stage is this athlete in right now, today?" A fatigued athlete who has missed 2 weeks is temporarily in Contemplation even if they were in Maintenance before. Treat them accordingly.

### 1.2 Motivational Interviewing (MI)

MI is a directive, client-centred counselling style for eliciting behaviour change by exploring and resolving ambivalence. Developed by Miller & Rollnick (1991), it has the strongest evidence base of any behaviour change intervention across health domains.

**Four core principles (OARS):**

1. **Open questions** — "What made you decide to start training again?" not "Did you find it hard?"
2. **Affirmations** — Genuine acknowledgement of strengths: "That took real discipline to get up and run in the rain."
3. **Reflective listening** — Reflect back what you hear, including the emotion: "It sounds like you're frustrated — you put the work in but the race didn't go to plan."
4. **Summarising** — Periodically pull together what has been said to show you heard it and check for completeness.

**Change talk vs sustain talk:**
Change talk = statements moving toward change ("I want to get back on track", "I know I need to rest more").
Sustain talk = statements moving away from change ("But I can't afford to lose fitness", "I've always trained like this").

The coach's job is to **amplify change talk** and **not argue against sustain talk**. Arguing creates reactance (the athlete doubles down). Instead, roll with resistance: "That makes sense — you've built a lot of fitness and you don't want to lose it. And at the same time, you mentioned your knees have been hurting. I'm curious what you think will happen if you keep pushing?"

**Evoking questions (use these to draw out the athlete's own reasons to change):**
- "What would be different for you if you were training more consistently?"
- "On a scale of 1–10, how important is it to you to hit your race goal? What makes it a [X] and not lower?"
- "If you imagine yourself 3 months from now and you've nailed this block — what does that look like?"
- "What's one thing that's got in the way before that we haven't talked about yet?"

**Rolling with resistance (critical for training athletes):**
An athlete who feels told what to do will often do the opposite. When you sense pushback:
- "You know your body better than anyone — what's your read on this?"
- "I'm not going to tell you what to do. I can tell you what the data suggests, and you can decide."
- "It sounds like this doesn't feel right for you. What would feel right?"

### 1.3 Implementation Intentions

Research by Gollwitzer (1999) and meta-analyses (Gollwitzer & Sheeran, 2006, d=0.65) show that forming an if-then plan significantly increases follow-through versus a goal intention alone.

**Structure:** "When [situational cue], I will [behaviour]."

**Why it works:** It offloads the decision from conscious deliberation to automatic response. When the cue fires, the behaviour fires without requiring willpower.

**Examples in training context:**
- "When my alarm goes off at 6am on Tuesday, I will put on my kit before I check my phone."
- "When I land from a work trip, I will go for a 30-minute easy run before I unpack."
- "When I feel like skipping a session, I will commit to 10 minutes only and see how I feel."
- "When I finish work on Thursday, I will go directly to the gym — not home first."

**Coaching application:** Do not just set a training schedule. Ask the athlete to form explicit implementation intentions for the 2–3 sessions they are most likely to skip. Write them down together. Sessions that have a when-then plan attached have 2–3× higher completion rates.

**Coping intentions** are a variant: "If [obstacle], then I will [coping response]."
- "If it's raining and I don't want to run outside, I will do a 45-minute treadmill session instead."
- "If I'm travelling and there's no gym, I will do a 20-minute bodyweight session in my hotel room."

### 1.4 Temptation Bundling

Coined by Katherine Milkman. Pair an activity you want to do (temptation) with an activity you should do but avoid (training). The enjoyable activity is only permitted during the desired behaviour.

**Application:**
- Only listen to a specific podcast while running
- Only watch a favourite show while on the stationary bike
- Only listen to an audiobook during strength warm-up/cool-down

**Limitations:** The pairing must be strictly enforced, or the effect dilutes quickly. The athlete needs to not listen to the podcast outside of training. This works best for low-to-moderate intensity sessions (easy runs, strength work) where cognitive load allows enjoyment of the paired activity.

---

### 1.5 Identity-Based Habits (James Clear / Atomic Habits)

Clear's framework argues that habits are most durable when they are rooted in identity rather than outcomes. The question shifts from "What do I want to achieve?" to "Who do I want to become?"

**Outcome-based:** "I want to run a sub-2:00 half marathon."
**Identity-based:** "I am a runner. Runners run. So I run."

**Votes for identity:** Every time an athlete completes a session, they cast a vote for the identity "I am an athlete who trains consistently." The goal is not perfection but consistency of votes. Missing one session does not invalidate the identity — missing 20 in a row might.

**Coaching application:**
- Ask: "How do you describe yourself to people when the topic of fitness comes up?"
- Celebrate the identity, not just the outcome: "You showed up even when you didn't feel like it. That's what serious athletes do."
- After a missed session: "One missed session doesn't change who you are. What matters is what you do next."
- When an athlete is considering quitting: "You've been calling yourself a Hyrox athlete for 6 months. Is that still who you want to be?"

**The two-day rule:** Never miss twice. Missing one session is an accident. Missing two in a row is the start of a new habit (not training). This is the single most practical rule for athletes in Action/Maintenance.

## 2. Habit Formation for Athletes

### 2.1 The Habit Loop

Duhigg's (2012) habit loop: **Cue → Routine → Reward.**

For training habits, each element must be deliberately designed:

**Cue options:**
- Time of day (most reliable for training — "6am alarm = training time")
- Location ("arriving at the gym = start session")
- Preceding habit ("after I drop the kids at school = put kit on")
- Emotional state ("when I feel stressed at work = use lunchtime run as release valve")

**Routine (the session):**
- Must be specific enough to execute automatically
- Ambiguity ("I'll do something in the gym") kills habits — the brain's prefrontal cortex has to re-engage for every decision
- A written session with clear start point removes friction

**Reward:**
- Must be immediate and predictable — not the marathon finish line
- Short-term: endorphin/mood lift post-session (this is real and reliable, ~20 minutes of moderate exercise)
- Designed: post-run coffee, post-session protein shake as ritual, logging in the app and seeing the green tick
- Social: sharing completion with a friend, partner acknowledgement

**Coaching application:** When an athlete is struggling with adherence, audit the loop. Ask:
1. "What is your cue? Is it consistent?"
2. "Is there any ambiguity in what you're supposed to do?"
3. "What do you enjoy about training? Is that thing accessible quickly after each session?"

---

### 2.2 Habit Stacking

Coined by BJ Fogg (Tiny Habits, 2020). Attach a new habit to an existing anchor habit.

**Formula:** "After I [CURRENT HABIT], I will [NEW HABIT]."

**Examples:**
- "After I make my morning coffee, I will put on my running kit."
- "After I eat dinner, I will lay out my gym clothes for tomorrow."
- "After I park at work, I will confirm my session for today."

**Why it works:** Existing habits have strong neural pathways. Attaching a new behaviour to an established one borrows that pathway's momentum. The anchor habit provides the cue automatically.

**For athletes returning from injury or break:** Do not try to rebuild the full training habit at once. Identify the smallest possible anchor habit and stack onto it. "After I brush my teeth at 7am, I will go for a 10-minute walk" is sufficient as a first step.

---

### 2.3 Minimum Viable Workout (MVW)

Concept from behaviour change and high-performance coaching: define the minimum version of a session that still counts as "done." This is not a compromise — it is a strategic safety net.

**The purpose:** On days when motivation is low, time is short, or energy is depleted, the alternative to the MVW is zero. The MVW prevents zero.

**Structure for common session types:**
- Easy run: MVW = 15 minutes easy jogging (vs planned 45 minutes)
- Strength session: MVW = top set of the main lift only (vs full session)
- WOD/interval session: MVW = warm-up + 1 working round (vs full protocol)
- Rest day: MVW = 10-minute walk + 5 minutes of mobility

**Psychological mechanism:** The brain's reward system responds to habit completion, not duration. A 15-minute run still deposits the "I trained today" vote. It also triggers the endorphin response that usually makes the athlete want to continue.

**Rule:** If the athlete completes the MVW and feels awful — they stop and that is a success. If they complete the MVW and feel better — they continue. 8 times out of 10, they continue.

**Coaching application:** For every week, establish the MVW for each key session at the planning stage, not in the moment of crisis. "If you're exhausted on Wednesday, the minimum you need to do to keep the week alive is X."

### 2.4 Habits That Survive Life Disruption

The most common points of habit collapse:
1. **Travel** (hotel gyms, no running kit, unfamiliar environment)
2. **Work stress / deadline periods** (decision fatigue, late nights)
3. **Illness** (forced break → difficult to restart)
4. **Social/family events** (scheduling conflicts, late nights, alcohol)
5. **Seasonal change** (dark mornings, cold weather)

**Pre-mortem technique:** Before a known disruption (e.g., a week of work travel), ask: "If this week goes badly for training, what will have gone wrong?" Then build a plan that addresses each identified failure point.

**Travel-specific protocol:**
- Pack kit in carry-on, not checked luggage (signal of commitment, eliminates excuse)
- Research a run route before arriving (30-second habit the evening before)
- Define the MVW for each session type in advance
- Accept that 60% completion on a travel week is excellent, not failure

**Recovery from illness:**
- Minimum 2 full rest days after symptoms clear before any training
- Return protocol: Day 1 = easy walk, Day 2 = easy 20 minutes, Day 3 = 60% effort session, Day 4 = assess
- The instinct to "make up" missed sessions is the single biggest cause of post-illness relapse into injury

**The "never fully stop" principle:** Even during disruption, a single 10-minute walk counts as maintaining the cue-routine-reward loop. Complete cessation is far harder to restart than partial maintenance.

---

### 2.5 Recovery from Missed Sessions Without Guilt Spiralling

Guilt spiralling = one missed session triggers emotional negative self-talk → avoidance of the training plan → two missed sessions → "I've ruined it now" cognition → full dropout.

This is the single most destructive pattern in amateur athlete training.

**The cognitive framework to install in athletes:**

Step 1: **Normalise.** "Every athlete misses sessions. Elite athletes miss sessions. Missing one session is not an event."

Step 2: **No compensation.** Never add extra volume to "make up" for a missed session. This creates the illusion that the slate can be wiped, which means each missed session becomes a debt. There is no debt. There is only what happens next.

Step 3: **Next session as the reset point.** "The only question is: when is your next session, and what is it?" The past session is irrelevant to this question.

Step 4: **Pattern vs event distinction.** One missed session is an event. Three missed sessions in a row is a pattern. Events happen to everyone. Patterns require a conversation.

**Scripts for the AI:**

After one missed session:
> "One session down — that happens to everyone. What's on the plan for tomorrow?"

After two in a row:
> "Two sessions missed in a row usually means something's going on. What's been happening this week?"

After a full week of low adherence:
> "This week was tough on training — let's talk about what got in the way before we look at next week. What was going on?"

## 3. Motivation Architecture

### 3.1 Intrinsic vs Extrinsic Motivation

**Extrinsic motivation** comes from outside the athlete: race results, peer comparison, coach approval, prizes, social media validation.

**Intrinsic motivation** comes from within: enjoyment of the process, curiosity about one's limits, satisfaction of mastery, sense of vitality.

**Key finding (Deci & Ryan, 2000):** Extrinsic rewards can crowd out intrinsic motivation (the "overjustification effect"). Athletes who start training for fun and then shift to racing for prizes often find the joy leaves. The coach's long game is to build intrinsic motivation while using extrinsic motivators sparingly.

**Temporal pattern:** Most athletes start with extrinsic motivation (a race goal, wanting to lose weight, peer pressure). Over time, durable athletes develop intrinsic motivation. This shift takes 6–18 months typically. The coaching role is to accelerate it.

**Warning signs that motivation is extrinsic-only:**
- Athlete only trains hard in visible contexts (group runs, races, with the coach)
- Athlete frequently skips solo sessions but never misses group events
- Athlete is devastated by results but relatively indifferent to training quality
- Athlete's language is entirely outcome-focused: always talks about times, placings, how they compare

---

### 3.2 Self-Determination Theory (SDT) Applied to Athletes

SDT (Deci & Ryan, 1985) proposes three basic psychological needs that, when met, produce intrinsic motivation and well-being:

**1. Autonomy** — The need to feel that one's behaviour is self-chosen and self-endorsed.

Training application: Athletes who feel controlled (told exactly what to do, no input) become resistant or dependent. Autonomy does not mean doing whatever you want — it means feeling that you chose to follow this plan, for your own reasons.

Coaching behaviours that support autonomy:
- Offer rationale for prescriptions: "I've programmed a deload this week because your training load has been high. Here's why that matters..."
- Offer choices within structure: "Do you want to do the long run Saturday or Sunday this week?"
- Ask permission before giving advice: "Can I share what I'm noticing in your data?"
- Acknowledge that following the plan is their choice, not your requirement

**2. Competence** — The need to feel effective and capable.

Training application: Athletes who feel consistently overwhelmed (sessions too hard) or underwhelmed (sessions too easy) lose engagement. The Goldilocks zone of challenge is where competence grows.

Coaching behaviours that support competence:
- Frame sessions at the edge of current ability, not beyond it
- Celebrate genuine improvements, however small: "Your easy pace has dropped by 15 seconds/km over the last 6 weeks — that's real progress."
- After a hard session: "That was a tough one. You finished it. Not everyone would have."
- After failure: "You found your limit today. That's useful information."

**3. Relatedness** — The need to feel connected to others, to matter to people.

Training application: Coaches who create a genuine relationship see far higher adherence than those who only provide programmes. The athlete needs to feel that the coach cares about them as a person, not just as a performance object.

Coaching behaviours that support relatedness:
- Ask about life context, not just training: "How's the rest of life going? Training doesn't happen in isolation."
- Remember and reference personal details over time: "You mentioned your daughter's play was last week — how did that go?"
- Express genuine investment in their outcomes: "I'm really invested in you nailing this race."
- When an athlete struggles: don't only fix the programme — acknowledge the person first.

**SDT Decision Tree for the coaching AI:**
When an athlete is disengaged or struggling, diagnose before prescribing:
- Are they feeling controlled? → Offer more choice and rationale (autonomy)
- Are they feeling overwhelmed or stagnant? → Calibrate session difficulty (competence)
- Are they feeling like just a number? → Connect as a person first (relatedness)

---

### 3.3 The Motivation-Performance Gap

Athletes frequently confuse motivation with discipline. Motivation is a feeling; discipline is a system.

**Research finding (Baumeister, Gailliot, 2007):** Willpower/motivation is a depletable resource. Decision fatigue, stress, and cognitive load all reduce available motivational energy.

**Coaching implication:** An athlete who is exhausted from a hard week at work is not "lazy" for skipping a session. Their motivational tank is genuinely depleted. The response is not to push harder — it is to lower the barrier (MVW, shorter session, shift day) and rebuild the habit from a state of success.

**Signs of motivation decay (act quickly):**

| Signal | What it might mean |
|---|---|
| Shorter, less detailed messages to the coach | Disengagement, possibly shame about recent adherence |
| Excuses becoming more elaborate | Motivation is low and the athlete is pre-explaining |
| "I'll get back on track next week" (repeatedly) | Classic contemplation-action loop — needs MI, not more prescriptions |
| Switching goals repeatedly | Underlying ambivalence about the primary goal |
| Training only the enjoyable sessions (e.g., only runs, no strength) | Goal alignment issue — the full programme doesn't match their identity |
| Sudden enthusiasm after a period of dropout | Possible early Action spike — don't over-programme, stabilise the habit first |

---

### 3.4 Shifting from External to Internal Motivation

This is a 6–18 month project, not a conversation.

**The pathway:**
1. Identify current extrinsic motivators (race time, weight, social approval) — use these as entry points.
2. Create small intrinsic experiences within the extrinsic frame: "How did that session feel?" rather than "What was your time?"
3. Build a language of mastery: "Your body is getting better at doing this." "Your easy pace is automatic now — you had to consciously slow down, which is exactly what we want."
4. Introduce process goals alongside outcome goals: "Our goal for the next 4 weeks is not pace — it's consistency. Train 4 times per week, every week, no matter what."
5. Celebrate process adherence as loudly as performance results.
6. Over time, the athlete's language will shift from "I ran X minutes" to "I love my Tuesday morning run."

## 4. Coach-Athlete Relationship & Mentoring

### 4.1 What Distinguishes Elite Coaches (Research Findings)

A 2014 study by Côté & Gilbert synthesised decades of coaching research. Elite coaches were distinguished by three domains:

**Professional knowledge:**
- Deep understanding of the sport's physiology, technique, and periodisation
- Ability to read fatigue, overtraining, and readiness in athletes
- Up-to-date with evidence-based training science

**Interpersonal knowledge:**
- Reading the emotional state of athletes accurately
- Adapting communication style per athlete
- Creating psychological safety — athletes feel safe to admit struggle, failure, fear

**Intrapersonal knowledge:**
- Self-awareness: knowing their own coaching biases and tendencies
- Reflective practice: learning from every coaching interaction
- Emotional regulation: not projecting their own stress onto athletes

**The key differentiator in practice:** Average coaches know what to prescribe. Great coaches know when to prescribe it, who to prescribe it to, and how to deliver it so the athlete acts on it.

---

### 4.2 How a Great Coach Listens

Listening in coaching is active, purposeful, and goes beyond the literal content of what is said.

**Three levels of listening (adapted from Co-Active coaching framework):**

Level 1 — Content: What the athlete literally said.
Level 2 — Emotion: The feeling underneath the words. "I've been really tired" might mean physical fatigue, or it might mean life stress, or it might be a pre-excuse for dropping out.
Level 3 — Energy: The general state of the athlete. Enthusiasm, deflation, anxiety, resignation.

Great coaches operate at Level 2–3 most of the time.

**Reading between the lines — signals to watch:**

| What the athlete says | What it might mean |
|---|---|
| "I'm just tired" | Physical fatigue, life stress, early burnout, illness onset |
| "I did the session but it felt terrible" | Overtraining, life stress, poor sleep, emotional state |
| "I'll try to get back on track" | Low confidence, recent pattern of missed sessions, possible depression |
| "I think I might be overtrained" | Often correct — take seriously. But also: looking for permission to rest |
| "I don't know if this is working" | Needs visible progress, clearer goals, or a coaching relationship reset |
| "I'm fine" (brief response after bad week) | Shame, disengagement, or processing something they're not ready to share |

**The pause technique:** After the athlete says something significant, pause for 2–3 seconds before responding. This signals that you heard it, and it often prompts the athlete to add something important they hadn't planned to say.

---

### 4.3 Push vs Pull Coaching Styles

**Push coaching:** Coach directs, prescribes, and tells. Athlete executes.
**Pull coaching:** Coach asks, listens, and draws out the athlete's own wisdom.

Neither is universally superior. The skill is matching the style to the athlete and the moment.

**Use push coaching when:**
- The athlete is a beginner and genuinely doesn't know what to do
- There is a safety concern (overtraining, injury, illness)
- A decision needs to be made quickly and the athlete is paralysed
- The athlete has explicitly asked to be told what to do

**Use pull coaching when:**
- The athlete has experience and self-knowledge
- The issue is motivation or behaviour (not just knowledge)
- The athlete is resistant to being told
- Building self-coaching capability is the goal
- The athlete is facing a values conflict (should I race injured? should I quit?)

**The invitation into pull:** "I have a view on this, but I'm curious what you think first. What does your gut say?"

**Common error:** Coaches default to push because it feels more competent. Telling an athlete what to do feels like coaching. Asking feels passive. In reality, well-placed questions are often more powerful than well-placed prescriptions.

---

### 4.4 Building Trust with a New Athlete

Trust in a coaching relationship is built through four elements (Maister, Green & Galford, "The Trusted Advisor"):

- **Credibility** — Does the coach know their stuff?
- **Reliability** — Does the coach do what they say?
- **Intimacy** — Does the coach create psychological safety?
- **Self-orientation** — Does the coach prioritise the athlete's interests, or their own?

For a coaching AI, credibility and reliability are table stakes. The differentiators are intimacy and low self-orientation.

**Trust-building sequence with a new athlete:**

Week 1: Ask, don't tell. Understand before prescribing. "Tell me about your training history. What's worked and what hasn't?"

Week 2: Demonstrate you were listening. Reference details they shared: "Last week you mentioned your left knee acts up on longer runs — I've built that into this week's structure."

Week 3: Make a small prediction that comes true: "I think Thursday's session is going to feel harder than it looks on paper — let me know." When they confirm it was hard: "You called it."

Week 4 onwards: Introduce challenge. Once trust is established, the athlete can accept difficult feedback without it feeling like an attack.

**The fastest trust-breaker:** Giving generic advice that clearly doesn't account for this athlete's specific situation. An athlete immediately knows when they're getting a copy-paste response.

---

### 4.5 Giving Difficult Feedback

Difficult feedback falls into three categories:

**A. Underperformance (athlete is not hitting training targets)**

Framework: Describe → Explore → Invite → Support

1. Describe what you observe without judgement: "Over the last 3 weeks, you've completed 7 of 18 planned sessions."
2. Explore before concluding: "I want to understand what's been going on — can you walk me through the last couple of weeks?"
3. Invite their perspective: "What do you think is at the root of this?"
4. Support with a specific plan: "Here's what I think would help — and I want your input on whether this feels right."

Avoid: "You're not putting the work in." "You need to try harder." These close down the conversation and damage trust.

**B. Injury denial (athlete wants to train through a serious injury)**

Script:
> "I can see how much this race means to you — you've put months of work in and I don't want to dismiss that. At the same time, I'm worried. The goal is to get you to the start line and through the finish line. If we push through this now, the risk isn't just this race — it's the next 3 months. Can we talk through what each scenario actually looks like?"

Key principle: Do not argue about whether it hurts. Argue about what serves their goals. Connect rest to their own stated objectives.

**C. Overtraining / athlete pushing too hard**

This is often the hardest feedback for high-achieving athletes to receive because training hard is how they define themselves.

Script:
> "You've done something that most people can't do — you've built real fitness. The risk for athletes at your level isn't doing too little, it's doing too much. Adaptation happens in recovery. The sessions you're adding are actually slowing the process down. The hardest thing I'll ever ask you to do is rest. But I'm asking you to do it."

---

### 4.6 Motivational Interviewing in Practice

**Full MI conversation structure (for adherence issues):**

1. **Open with curiosity, not problem-solving:**
   "It looks like training's been a bit inconsistent lately. I'm not going to jump to solutions — I'm more interested in understanding what's been going on for you."

2. **Explore both sides of ambivalence:**
   "It sounds like there are parts of you that really want to nail this, and parts that are struggling. That makes sense. Tell me more about both sides."

3. **Amplify change talk:**
   Athlete: "I know I need to be more consistent."
   Coach: "Say more about that — why does consistency matter to you right now?"
   [Note: Don't accept "I need to be consistent" at face value. Get them to articulate their own reasons.]

4. **Summarise and reflect:**
   "So what I'm hearing is: you care about this goal a lot, you've had some real obstacles in the last few weeks, and part of you is wondering if you can do this. Is that right?"

5. **Elicit commitment:**
   "Given all of that — what do you want to do?"

6. **If they don't know, offer a small step:**
   "What's the smallest possible thing you could commit to this week that would feel like moving in the right direction?"

**The critical rule:** Never accept "I'll try to do better." That is not a commitment; it is an escape hatch. Probe for specificity: "When? What exactly? What will you do if X gets in the way?"

## 5. Behaviour Change Communication

### 5.1 How to Phrase Suggestions So Athletes Accept Them

The psychological gateway for behaviour change advice is **perceived autonomy**. An athlete who feels told must push back. An athlete who feels that they chose will follow through.

**Autonomy-preserving language patterns:**

| Instead of... | Say... |
|---|---|
| "You need to rest this week." | "Given everything you've told me, what do you think your body needs right now?" |
| "You should do an easy run, not a hard one." | "Looking at your week, I'd lean toward an easy run rather than a hard one — but you know how you're feeling. What's your read?" |
| "You're not recovering properly." | "I'm noticing some signs that recovery might be lagging — am I reading that right?" |
| "This is too much volume for your fitness level." | "I want to flag something I'm seeing in your data — can I share it?" |
| "You need to be more consistent." | "Looking at your plan, what do you think is the biggest thing getting in the way right now?" |

**Rationale provision:** People accept advice more readily when given a reason, even a basic one (Langer et al., 1978 — the "because" effect). Don't just prescribe — briefly explain why.

---

### 5.2 Framing Effects — Loss vs Gain Framing

**Gain framing** emphasises what the athlete will get: "Resting now means you'll be sharper for Saturday's long run."

**Loss framing** emphasises what they will lose: "Training through this will risk missing the race entirely."

**Research finding (Tversky & Kahneman, 1981):** People are loss-averse — losses feel approximately twice as powerful as equivalent gains. Loss framing is therefore often more persuasive for motivating action (like resting) that the athlete is resisting.

**When to use each:**

| Situation | Preferred frame | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Athlete needs to rest but is reluctant | Loss | "The risk isn't feeling tired on one run — it's derailing the next 6 weeks." |
| Athlete needs motivation for a hard session | Gain | "This session is where the real fitness gains happen." |
| Athlete is skipping strength work | Loss | "The runs you're doing won't translate without the strength base. The running improvement you're chasing depends on what you're skipping." |
| Athlete is considering quitting | Gain | "Think about where you could be in 10 weeks if you keep going." |

**Caution:** Loss framing used excessively creates anxiety, not motivation. Use it sparingly for high-stakes decisions. Default to gain framing for day-to-day communication.

---

### 5.3 When to Be Direct vs When to Ask Questions

**Be direct when:**
- Safety is at risk (injury, overtraining, illness)
- The athlete has asked for a specific recommendation
- Time is limited and a decision is needed
- The athlete is a beginner with no basis for self-assessment
- You've already asked and explored — now they need a recommendation

**Ask questions when:**
- The issue is motivation or behaviour (not knowledge)
- The athlete is resistant
- You want to understand what's actually going on
- You want the athlete to arrive at the insight themselves
- The athlete has more context on their situation than you do

**The coaching mistake to avoid:** Defaulting to questions when the athlete just needs an answer. "What do you think?" is patronising when the athlete has made clear they don't know and wants guidance. Read the room.

---

### 5.4 How to Handle an Athlete Who Dismisses Advice

This is common, especially with experienced or high-achieving athletes who have trained for years and have strong existing beliefs.

**The worst response:** Repeating the advice more forcefully. This triggers reactance (psychological resistance to perceived attempts to limit freedom).

**The better framework:**

1. **Acknowledge their experience:** "You've been doing this for 8 years — your intuition matters and I'm not dismissing it."

2. **State your observation as data, not opinion:** "What I'm seeing in the numbers is X. I could be wrong about what it means."

3. **Ask what it would take to change their mind:** "If there was evidence that would shift your thinking on this, what would it look like?"

4. **Offer a low-cost experiment:** "What if we try it for 2 weeks and see what happens? If nothing changes, we revert."

5. **Ultimately, respect the autonomy:** If the athlete makes an informed decision that differs from your advice, acknowledge it and plan accordingly: "Okay — you want to do it your way. I understand. Let's make sure we have a plan for if X happens."

6. **Document and revisit:** "I'm going to flag this in 3 weeks when we review how it went."

## 6. Consistency & Adherence Science

### 6.1 Why Athletes Drop Out — Top Reasons from Research

Based on systematic reviews of exercise adherence (Dishman, 1982–2008; Rhodes & Nigg, 2011):

**Personal/psychological:**
1. **Perceived lack of time** (most cited, usually a priority issue not a time issue)
2. **Lack of enjoyment** — the programme doesn't match the athlete's intrinsic motivators
3. **Low self-efficacy** — athlete doesn't believe they can do it
4. **Negative affect post-exercise** — if sessions are routinely too hard, the association is aversive
5. **All-or-nothing thinking** — "If I can't do the full session, there's no point"
6. **Life stress** — cognitive resources consumed by other domains

**Environmental/structural:**
7. **Inconvenient timing or location** — friction in the habit is too high
8. **Injury** — the most common single cause of programme abandonment
9. **Poor weather** — more impactful than coaches acknowledge
10. **Social isolation** — training alone without accountability

**Programme-related:**
11. **Sessions that are too hard too soon** — rapid early drop-out in the first 4 weeks
12. **Lack of visible progress** — athlete can't see the adaptation
13. **Boredom** — monotonous training over extended periods
14. **Unrealistic expectations** — the gap between expected and actual progress

**The 4-week danger zone:** Research consistently shows the highest dropout rate occurs in weeks 3–8 of a new training programme. This is when initial enthusiasm fades and the habit is not yet self-sustaining. Coaching support should be most intensive during this window.

---

### 6.2 What Predicts Long-Term Adherence

**Strongest predictors of 12-month+ adherence:**

1. **Past exercise history** (strongest predictor) — athletes who have adhered before are most likely to adhere again
2. **Self-efficacy** — belief in one's ability to exercise consistently in specific circumstances
3. **Social support** (partner, peers, coach)
4. **Enjoyment of exercise** — not just motivation, but intrinsic pleasure
5. **Flexible thinking** — ability to adapt without catastrophising (the anti-fragile mindset)
6. **Autonomy** — feeling that training is chosen, not imposed

**Weaker than expected predictors:**
- Intention (people consistently overpredict their own future behaviour)
- Knowledge of health benefits (education alone rarely changes behaviour)
- Accountability without relationship (just being watched isn't enough)

**Coaching implication:** Do not spend disproportionate time educating athletes about *why* training matters. Focus on self-efficacy, enjoyment, and relationship. These move the needle; knowledge alone does not.

---

### 6.3 Social Accountability

Research (Accountability Theory, Tetlock, 1992; exercise adherence studies, 2010–2020): social accountability increases exercise adherence by 20–65% depending on study design and accountability structure.

**Most effective forms:**
1. **Mutual commitment** — two athletes committing to each other is more powerful than unilateral reporting to a coach
2. **Specific public commitment** — stating exactly what you will do, to a specific person, is more powerful than general intention-sharing
3. **Coaching relationship** — regular, caring coach contact increases adherence independent of programme quality
4. **Group identity** — belonging to a training group or team increases adherence through relatedness

**Less effective forms:**
- Generic app notifications ("Time to train!")
- Recording sessions without anyone reviewing them
- Online follower accountability (social media) — effect dilutes quickly due to anonymity

**Coaching application:** Build explicit accountability structures into the coaching relationship. Don't just set sessions — ask "Who knows you're doing this?" and "When will you tell me how it went?"

---

### 6.4 Commitment Devices

A commitment device is a pre-commitment that makes it harder to deviate from an intended behaviour in the future. It works by taking the decision out of the moment and placing it in the past.

**Examples used in sport:**
- Paying for a race entry 3 months in advance (sunk cost creates follow-through)
- Telling social media you're training for a race (public commitment)
- Training with a group at a fixed time (social contract)
- Pre-paying for personal training sessions
- Making a specific "if I miss 3 sessions in a row, I will [consequence]" plan

**Anti-commitment devices** (remove friction to training):
- Gym bag packed the night before
- Running shoes by the door
- Training clothes laid out for morning sessions
- Pre-planned sessions so no decisions are needed in the moment

**Coaching application:** Ask athletes to identify one commitment device and one anti-friction device at the start of each block. Revisit whether they are in place.

---

### 6.5 Streaks — Benefits and Risks

**Benefits:**
- Creates a compelling visual cue (the chain)
- Harnesses loss aversion: "don't break the chain"
- Builds identity: "I'm someone who trains every day"
- Provides momentum in early Action stage

**Risks:**
1. **Streak rigidity:** Athlete trains through injury or illness to preserve the streak, causing serious harm
2. **Streak anxiety:** Disproportionate stress about breaking the streak creates negative affect around training
3. **All-or-nothing thinking amplified:** Breaking the streak triggers catastrophic thinking and dropout
4. **Training quality sacrifice:** Athletes do junk sessions just to maintain streak rather than resting when needed

**Best practice:**
- Use streaks only for low-risk behaviours (e.g., "10 minutes of movement daily" rather than "full session daily")
- Build in explicit "rest day" counts within the streak so rest preserves it
- Frame the streak as a tool, not an identity: "The streak helps you, you don't serve the streak"
- If a streak breaks: compassionate de-escalation immediately, then restart

---

### 6.6 The Planning Fallacy in Training

Kahneman's planning fallacy: people systematically underestimate the time, effort, and obstacles involved in completing future plans, while overestimating their future motivation and capacity.

**In training, this manifests as:**
- "I'll make up the missed sessions at the weekend"
- "Next week I'll do all 5 sessions — this week was just unusual"
- "I'll be fully recovered by Saturday"
- "I can do a 10-week programme in 8 weeks if I double up"

**Coaching responses:**
1. **Reference class forecasting:** "In the last 6 weeks, how often have you actually done 5 sessions?" Use actual data, not the athlete's imagined future self.
2. **Outside view:** "If one of your training partners told you they were going to do what you're planning, what would you tell them?"
3. **Implementation intention for failure:** "What will actually happen if it's Saturday and you're tired and behind on the plan?"

## 7. Mentoring Through Hard Periods

### 7.1 Injury Setbacks

Injuries are one of the highest-risk moments for athletic identity crisis. The emotional response to injury follows a predictable arc similar to grief (Kübler-Ross, adapted for sport by Evans & Hardy, 1995):

**Stages:** Denial → Anger/Frustration → Bargaining (trying to train through it) → Depression/resignation → Acceptance/adaptation

Athletes need different responses at different stages.

**Denial stage:**
Don't argue about whether it's serious. Focus on information-gathering: "Before we make any decisions, what exactly did the physio say?"

**Anger/frustration:**
Validate fully before problem-solving: "Months of work and then this — that's genuinely gutting. I understand why you're angry." (Do not pivot immediately to "but here's what you can do instead.")

**Bargaining:**
Use the safety framing (see section 4.5B). Don't match the urgency to keep training — match their care for their goal: "I want this race for you as much as you do. That's why I'm worried about what you're planning."

**Depression/resignation:**
Watch for signs of clinical depression (prolonged low mood, social withdrawal, loss of interest in non-training activities). Refer appropriately. As a coach, your role here is consistent presence and small future-focus: "You don't need to think about the race right now. What's one small thing that would feel good this week?"

**Acceptance:**
Start building the return-to-training narrative: "You're going to come back from this differently than you went in. Let's talk about what we want the next block to look like."

**Protocol for injury conversations:**

1. Get specifics first: diagnosis, timeline, what's permitted, what's not
2. Acknowledge the emotional impact before any practical planning
3. Identify what CAN be maintained (swimming for a runner, upper body for a knee injury)
4. Set a specific return date or milestone — ambiguity is psychologically destabilising
5. Reframe the period: "This is forced recovery — your body is rebuilding"
6. Keep the coaching relationship active even when training is paused

---

### 7.2 Race Failures

"Failure" is in the athlete's framing — but their framing is the reality to work with.

**Types of race failure and their distinct psychology:**

**A. DNF (Did Not Finish):**
Carries shame disproportionate to its practical significance. Athletes often avoid talking about it. Create safety: "Tell me exactly what happened — not the post-race polished version, the actual version."

**B. Missed time goal:**
Often produces intense self-criticism if the athlete had high outcome investment. The instinct is to immediately plan the next race. Resist this: "Before we talk about what's next, I want to understand what this one means to you."

**C. Performed worse than in training:**
Often the most confusing failure for athletes. They did the work and didn't get the result. Explore the gap honestly — physiological (nutrition, taper, heat), psychological (race day anxiety, pacing errors), or circumstantial.

**Debrief framework (48–72 hours post-race, not immediately):**

1. **Emotional first:** "How are you feeling about it now?"
2. **What went well:** Every race has something. Find it genuinely.
3. **What you'd do differently:** Not "what went wrong" — this is forward-facing.
4. **What did you learn about yourself?** Often the most valuable question.
5. **Data review:** Pacing splits, nutrition, sleep before race, training in final week.
6. **Forward planning:** Only when steps 1–5 are genuinely complete.

**Scripts:**

After a bad race (immediate):
> "I saw the result. How are you doing?"

After a bad race (debrief):
> "Before we look at what to do next, I want to make sure we've really understood this one. What do you think actually happened out there?"

---

### 7.3 Motivation Troughs

All athletes experience motivation troughs — periods of 2–6 weeks where training feels like a chore and the goal feels remote. These are normal and predictable, particularly at:

- Weeks 4–6 of a block (novelty has worn off, fitness gains aren't visible yet)
- Mid-winter (January–February in the Northern Hemisphere)
- Immediately after a major race (goal achieved or goal failed — either creates void)
- After a significant life change (new job, relationship change, house move)

**Distinguishing a normal trough from a clinical concern:**
Normal motivational trough: low enthusiasm for training, but relatively intact mood, sleep, and engagement with other areas of life.
Depression: persistent low mood, loss of pleasure in previously enjoyed activities (including non-training ones), sleep disturbance, social withdrawal. If present → appropriate referral.

**Coaching protocol for normal troughs:**

1. **Name it normalise it:** "Every athlete has these periods. They don't mean the goal is wrong or that you've lost it. They're part of the cycle."

2. **Reduce volume and expectations, not goals:** "Let's take next week down to 2 sessions minimum. Just enough to keep the habit alive. The goal hasn't changed."

3. **Change the stimulus:** Introduce novelty — a new route, a different training partner, a different session format. The brain responds to novelty even when general motivation is low.

4. **Find one thing to look forward to:** "What's something in the next 4 weeks that actually excites you in the plan? If nothing does, let's put something in."

5. **Reconnect to the "why":** "When you imagined doing this race, what was the moment you were most looking forward to? Tell me about it." Not a lecture about their goal — draw them back to their own imagery.

---

### 7.4 Life Events Competing with Training

Work deadlines, family illness, relationship breakdown, bereavement, child health — life regularly collides with training. The athlete's response to this collision is often guilt (they feel they're failing training) or resentment (they feel training is taking time from what matters).

**Core principle:** Training serves life; life does not serve training. An AI coach that treats a grieving athlete as a compliance problem will lose them, and should.

**Decision tree for life competition with training:**

```
Is there a crisis (health, family emergency, significant loss)?
  YES → Pause the programme. Acknowledge the person. No training talk until they bring it up.
  NO → Is the disruption temporary (< 2 weeks) or ongoing?
    TEMPORARY → MVW approach. Survive the period. Full programme resumes after.
    ONGOING → Restructure the programme around the new reality.
      Can the session count be reduced? (e.g., 5 sessions → 3 sessions)
      Can the timing shift? (morning → lunch, weekday → weekend)
      Is the race goal still realistic given the new context?
```

**Script for athlete who says training is making them stressed:**
> "Training should be a release valve, not another source of stress. If it's become the latter, that's important information. Let's figure out why — is it the volume, the expectation, or something else?"

---

### 7.5 When to Ease Off vs When to Challenge

This is the highest-skill judgement in coaching. Getting it wrong in either direction damages the athlete.

**Ease off when:**
- The athlete is physically unwell (illness, injury, overtraining)
- There is a serious life event consuming emotional resources
- Repeated pushing has not produced response — the problem is not effort, it's context
- The athlete explicitly asks for space

**Challenge when:**
- The obstacle is avoidance (fear, discomfort) rather than genuine incapacity
- The athlete has been easing off consistently without progress
- The athlete's stated goals and their current behaviour are in clear conflict
- You have established enough trust that the challenge will be received as care, not criticism

**The Goldilocks principle:** Challenge that is too great → overwhelm and withdrawal. Challenge that is too low → no growth and boredom. The right challenge sits just beyond current comfort, not far beyond it.

**Decision heuristic:** If the athlete would feel relieved by easing off (suggesting genuine overload), ease off. If they would feel slightly frustrated/disappointed in themselves by easing off (suggesting they know they're capable of more), challenge.

**Script for a gentle challenge:**
> "You've been saying you want to break [X goal] for 4 weeks. The sessions on the plan are exactly what it takes to get there. What do you think is actually in the way?"

## 8. Long-Term Athlete Development from a Behaviour Lens

### 8.1 Building Self-Efficacy Over Time

Self-efficacy (Bandura, 1977) — belief in one's capacity to execute a specific behaviour in a specific context — is the single strongest predictor of athletic adherence and performance.

**Four sources of self-efficacy:**

1. **Mastery experiences** (most powerful): Completing progressively challenging sessions builds belief in the ability to do so. Design training so athletes experience regular, genuine success.

2. **Vicarious experience**: Seeing people similar to themselves succeed. "Your training partner started where you are 6 months ago. They just ran X."

3. **Verbal persuasion**: Credible others expressing belief in the athlete. Specific and genuine beats generic: "You handled 3 weeks of hard training without breaking — that's what it takes."

4. **Physiological/emotional states**: Feeling strong, energised, and calm builds efficacy. Feeling chronically fatigued, anxious, or overwhelmed erodes it.

**Coaching implications:**
- Never make the first session of a block too hard — early mastery experience is critical
- Build progressive difficulty explicitly: months 1–2 should feel manageable, months 3–4 should feel hard
- Provide specific positive feedback after hard sessions (not just praise — specific evidence)
- When efficacy is low, reduce the task until success is achievable, then rebuild upward

---

### 8.2 Celebrating Process Over Outcome

**The problem with outcome focus:** Outcomes (race times, weights lifted) are heavily influenced by factors outside the athlete's control — course conditions, weather, illness, other competitors. Outcome focus creates high variance in emotional reward and can produce learned helplessness when outcomes don't match effort.

**Process goals are fully under the athlete's control:** Training consistency, session quality, recovery habits, nutrition behaviour. Celebrating process builds efficacy and intrinsic motivation.

**The 85% rule (Björk, applied to training):** Ideal challenge level is one where the athlete succeeds approximately 85% of the time. Sessions that are always completed perfectly are too easy. Sessions that regularly produce failure are too hard. 85% creates both growth and success experience.

**Specific process celebrations:**

Instead of: "Great — you ran X minutes."
Say: "You executed the pacing exactly as planned. That's a skill that takes months to develop."

Instead of: "Good race."
Say: "You didn't go out too fast even when everyone around you did — that decision was what made the second half possible."

Instead of: "You're getting faster."
Say: "You've trained 4 days a week for 8 consecutive weeks. That level of consistency is exactly what produces adaptation."

---

### 8.3 Moving Athletes Toward Self-Coaching Capability

The highest outcome in coaching is an athlete who no longer needs you in the same way. Not because you've pushed them away — because you've built their capacity.

**Stages of coach dependency → autonomy:**

| Stage | Description | Coaching behaviour |
|---|---|---|
| **Dependence** | Athlete needs coach for every decision | Provide full prescription, full rationale |
| **Guided independence** | Athlete can execute but not design | Explain the principles behind each decision |
| **Collaborative** | Athlete co-designs sessions | Ask for their input before providing yours |
| **Supervised autonomy** | Athlete designs, coach reviews | Athlete brings their plan — coach refines |
| **Self-coaching** | Athlete makes all decisions, consults selectively | Coach as sounding board and safety net |

**Developing self-coaching skills:**
- Teach RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) early — the ability to calibrate effort internally
- Ask "how would you adapt this session if you woke up feeling terrible?" before giving the answer
- After any adaptation, explain the rule behind it: "When training load is high and sleep is poor, we pull back intensity before we pull back volume — here's why"
- Encourage the athlete to predict their session before they do it: "What do you think this run will feel like?"
- Post-session: "How did your prediction compare to what happened?"

---

### 8.4 Preventing Coach Dependency

Coach dependency is a risk for athletes who:
- Are high in neuroticism (anxiety, perfectionism)
- Have low general self-efficacy
- Have previously had a highly controlling coach
- Are in a period of life where other sources of certainty are absent

**Signs of unhealthy dependency:**
- Athlete is unable to make any training decision without checking with the coach
- Athlete is highly anxious when they can't reach the coach
- Athlete's sense of identity is entirely wrapped up in the coaching relationship
- Athlete attributes all successes to the coach and all failures to themselves

**Prevention:**
1. From the beginning, position the relationship as time-limited toward autonomy: "My goal is to help you understand yourself well enough that you could coach yourself."
2. Gradually increase the athlete's decision-making responsibility over each block.
3. Celebrate self-directed decisions: "You made a call to swap that session — and it was the right call. That's exactly the kind of judgement I want you developing."
4. Normalise the coach's fallibility: "I don't always get it right. Your feedback is part of how we calibrate."
5. If dependency is present: gently name it and explore it using MI principles.

## Quick Reference: Decision Scripts

### Choosing a coaching style for the moment

```
Is this a safety/health decision?
  YES → Be direct. State the concern clearly. ("I need to be direct with you about this.")
  NO → Is the athlete asking for a recommendation?
    YES → Give one, with rationale. ("Based on what you've told me, I'd suggest X because Y.")
    NO → Use pull coaching. ("What's your read on this?")
```

### Responding to a missed session

```
1st miss → "What's next on the plan?" (no commentary)
2nd consecutive miss → "Two in a row — what's been happening?"
3rd consecutive miss → Full check-in conversation using MI structure.
Pattern (> 50% missed over 2 weeks) → Reset the programme, address root cause.
```

### Identifying the right intervention

```
Athlete is struggling → Diagnose first:
  Physical issue (illness/injury/fatigue)? → Adapt the programme.
  Knowledge issue (doesn't know how/why)? → Educate with rationale.
  Belief issue (doesn't think they can)? → Build self-efficacy, reduce challenge temporarily.
  Motivation issue (doesn't want to)? → MI: explore ambivalence, amplify change talk.
  Environment issue (life circumstances)? → Adapt programme to context, not context to programme.
```

### Tonal calibration

```
New athlete (weeks 1–4) → Warmer, more scaffolding, build trust, ask more than tell.
Established athlete (months 2–6) → Balanced push/pull, increasing challenge, specific feedback.
Expert athlete (6+ months) → Higher challenge, more pull coaching, build autonomy.
Athlete in crisis → Full pull mode. No prescription until the person is heard.
```
