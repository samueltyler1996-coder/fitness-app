# Hyrox Coaching Knowledge Base

_Reference document for the Hyrox coaching AI. All times, loads, and protocols reflect current Hyrox World Series standards as of 2025._

---

## 1. Race Format & Physiological Demands

### Exact Race Structure

A Hyrox race consists of **8 rounds**, each comprising:
1. A **1km run** (on an indoor track or looped course)
2. A **functional station** (performed in sequence, no rest between run finish and station start)

Total run distance: **8km**
Total station work: cumulative functional output across all 8 stations

The sequence is fixed and identical at every race worldwide:
1. 1km Run → SkiErg 1000m
2. 1km Run → Sled Push 50m
3. 1km Run → Sled Pull 50m
4. 1km Run → Burpee Broad Jump 80m
5. 1km Run → Rowing 1000m
6. 1km Run → Farmer's Carry 200m
7. 1km Run → Sandbag Lunges 100m
8. 1km Run → Wall Balls (75–100 reps depending on division)

### Loads by Division

| Division | SkiErg | Sled Push | Sled Pull | Burpee BJ | Row | Farmer's Carry | Sandbag Lunges | Wall Balls |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Open Women | 1000m | 60kg + sled | 60kg + sled | 80m | 1000m | 2×12kg | 10kg / 20m lunges × 5 = 100m | 4kg / 75 reps |
| Open Men | 1000m | 102kg + sled | 102kg + sled | 80m | 1000m | 2×24kg | 20kg / 75 reps WB | 6kg / 75 reps |
| Pro Women | 1000m | 102kg + sled | 102kg + sled | 80m | 1000m | 2×16kg | 10kg / 100 reps WB | 4kg / 100 reps |
| Pro Men | 1000m | 152kg + sled | 152kg + sled | 80m | 1000m | 2×32kg | 20kg / 100 reps WB | 6kg / 100 reps |

> Note: Sled load refers to plates added; the sled itself typically weighs ~20-25kg. Venue floors vary — AstroTurf is standard, adding significant friction. Loads above are plate loads as listed in race specs.

### Typical Finish Times

| Division | Elite | Competitive Age Grouper | First-Timer / Recreational |
|---|---|---|---|
| Open Men | sub-60 min | 70–85 min | 90–110 min |
| Open Women | sub-65 min | 75–95 min | 95–120 min |
| Pro Men | sub-55 min | 60–75 min | n/a (by qualifier) |
| Pro Women | sub-60 min | 65–75 min | n/a |

World record benchmarks (2025): Pro Men ~54–56 min; Pro Women ~60–62 min.

### What the Race Actually Tests

**Aerobic capacity (VO2max, ~60–70% of performance variance):** The 8km of running at a sustained effort above easy pace is the dominant time component. Athletes with a 5km PB under 20 minutes run the race categorically differently from those over 25 minutes.

**Lactate tolerance / lactate clearance:** Each station elevates lactate significantly. The ability to clear lactate during the subsequent 1km run — rather than accumulating it — is the key physiological differentiator between athletes who "come apart" after station 4 and those who maintain pace.

**Functional strength endurance:** Not maximal strength. The stations require sustained output against moderate-to-heavy loads for 2–8 minutes per station. Grip, posterior chain, and leg endurance matter more than 1RM numbers.

**Mental durability:** Hyrox hurts uniformly across all 8 rounds. The psychological skill of resetting after a hard station and committing to the next run is undercoached but critical.

## 2. The 8 Stations — Training & Standards

### Station 1: SkiErg 1000m

**Target Times by Level**

| Level | Time |
|---|---|
| Elite | sub-3:30 (men), sub-4:00 (women) |
| Competitive | 3:30–4:30 (men), 4:00–5:00 (women) |
| Recreational | 4:30–6:00 (men), 5:00–7:00 (women) |

**Technique Cues**
- Initiate pull from the lats and core — not arms. Arms follow the body.
- Hinge at the hip simultaneously with the pull; knees soft, not a deep squat.
- Full extension at the top: hands reach overhead, body tall before the next pull.
- Keep a consistent rhythm — do not sprint the first 200m. Settle at target pace by stroke 10.
- Damper setting: 3–5 for most athletes. Higher is not better; it slows stroke rate and increases muscle fatigue.
- Monitor stroke rate: 24–30 strokes/min for a sustainable 1000m race pace.

**Common Errors**
- Using arms only (no hip hinge) — dramatically increases arm fatigue and reduces power per stroke.
- Starting too fast — first 200m at 10+ seconds/500m faster than target, then dying over final 400m.
- Grip too tight — forearm pump impairs performance in subsequent carry and wall ball stations.
- Not using the leg drive at the base of the pull — the calves and quads should push off the floor.

**Training Protocols**
- **Volume base:** 3 × 500m @ race pace + 90 sec rest. Progress to 5 × 500m.
- **Threshold work:** 4 × 250m @ 5–8% faster than race pace / 45 sec rest.
- **Race simulation:** 1000m continuous at goal pace, immediately into a 400m run.
- **Weekly volume:** 3,000–6,000m total SkiErg in peak weeks.
- **Off-season transfer:** Cable pull-down rows, lat pull-downs, and banded pull-aparts build the pulling pattern.

### Station 2: Sled Push 50m

**Weight by Category** (see table above — Open Men 102kg plates, Pro Men 152kg plates)

**Target Times by Level**

| Level | Open Men | Open Women |
|---|---|---|
| Elite | sub-1:00 | sub-1:15 |
| Competitive | 1:00–1:45 | 1:15–2:00 |
| Recreational | 1:45–3:00 | 2:00–3:30 |

**Technique Cues**
- Hands on the upright posts, not the low horizontal bar — gives better leverage.
- Low hips, flat back, lean forward at approximately 45 degrees.
- Short, powerful strides — overstride and you lose drive angle.
- Head neutral (don't look up at the end of the lane — it kills posture).
- Drive through the full foot, not the toe.
- Breathe on the push phase, not holding breath for full 50m.

**How to Train It**
- If you have a sled: work up to race weight + 15–20% in training. 4–6 × 20m @ race weight with 90 sec rest.
- Without a sled: prowler push substitute — heavy barbell sled drag in a gym. Alternatively, heavy Romanian deadlift + forward lean walks build the hip drive pattern.
- **Specific gym transfers:** Low-bar back squat (builds posterior chain), hip thrust (builds glute drive), Bulgarian split squat (builds single-leg pushing strength).
- Aim to be able to back squat 1.5× bodyweight to handle race sled loads comfortably.

**Common Errors**
- Hips too high (effectively pushing with just upper body).
- Hands too low (changes drive angle, less leverage).
- Losing contact — small steps stop and restart momentum; keep continuous pressure.

### Station 3: Sled Pull 50m

**Target Times by Level** (approximately 15–20% slower than sled push at same weight)

| Level | Open Men | Open Women |
|---|---|---|
| Elite | sub-1:15 | sub-1:30 |
| Competitive | 1:15–2:00 | 1:30–2:15 |
| Recreational | 2:00–3:15 | 2:15–3:45 |

**Technique Cues**
- Rope pull technique: hand-over-hand, not alternating — faster.
- Sit back into a squat or hinge, using bodyweight to create tension, then pull with lat + arm.
- Keep the rope taut at all times — slack means wasted effort on the next pull.
- Footwork: walk backwards as you pull, keeping close to the rope line.
- Grip: use chalk where allowed. False grip (thumb over fingers) reduces forearm fatigue.

**Training Protocols**
- Rope pull sled in gym: 3–4 × 25m seated or standing, building to race weight.
- Without specific equipment: seated cable rows, heavy barbell rows, and face pulls.
- Grip training: dead hangs (60+ sec), towel pull-ups, thick bar deadlifts. Grip is the limiting factor for most athletes on the pull.
- **Key gym transfers:** Pendlay row, single-arm dumbbell row, trap bar deadlift.

### Station 4: Burpee Broad Jump 80m

**Target Times / Reps by Level**
Approximately 16–20 broad jumps cover 80m depending on height and jump distance (aim for 1.5–2m per jump).

| Level | Time for 80m |
|---|---|
| Elite | sub-3:30 |
| Competitive | 3:30–5:00 |
| Recreational | 5:00–7:30 |

**Pacing Strategy**
- This is the only station without equipment — therefore it cannot be scaled in difficulty. Most athletes underestimate it.
- Do NOT sprint the first 15–20 jumps. Set a rhythm and maintain it.
- Target jump technique: land feet together, not staggered. Push off both feet for maximum distance.
- Chest fully touches the floor on each burpee — no partial reps (referees watch closely).
- Arms sweep forward and overhead on the jump to gain distance.
- Pace guideline: aim for a consistent 6–8 burpee broad jumps per minute. Going faster early guarantees a wall in the final 20m.

**Training Progressions**
- Week 1–4: 3 × 15 BBJ for quality + distance, 90 sec rest.
- Week 5–8: 3 × 25 BBJ, reducing rest to 60 sec.
- Week 9–12: Race simulation: 40 BBJ continuous, then immediately 400m run.
- Max continuous set 2–3 weeks out: attempt 80m for time — record as benchmark.
- **Quality cue:** Every rep should be maximal distance. A fatigued athlete takes 25 jumps to cover 80m; a fresh athlete takes 16. Efficiency training matters more than cardio for this station.

**Maintaining Quality Under Fatigue**
- Break at a consistent rhythm: 5 reps, breath check, 5 reps. Avoid irregular panic breaks.
- Eyes forward, not down — keeps posture tall for jumping.
- If you stop, it takes 3–4 burpees to re-establish rhythm — breaks are expensive.

### Station 5: Rowing 1000m

**Target Times by Level**

| Level | Open Men | Open Women |
|---|---|---|
| Elite | sub-3:20 | sub-3:45 |
| Competitive | 3:20–4:00 | 3:45–4:30 |
| Recreational | 4:00–5:30 | 4:30–6:00 |

**Technique Under Fatigue**
- In a race, you arrive at the rower after 4 runs and 4 stations. Technique degrades predictably.
- Priority order when tired: (1) leg drive, (2) layback, (3) arm pull. Arms last, legs first — always.
- Catch: shin vertical before the drive. Do not shoot the seat before the hands.
- Set damper to 4–5 for most athletes in race conditions. Higher drag = more effort per stroke.
- Start with 20–22 strokes/min for first 200m, then settle to 24–26 for the remainder.
- Split target: if goal is 4:00 total, target 2:00/500m consistently. Do not negative split aggressively — even splits are better than going out slow.

**Relationship to Run Pacing**
- The row is the second major aerobic station (after SkiErg). Athletes with a 5km run time under 23 min should target 4:00 or faster on the row.
- Row and SkiErg performance correlates ~0.85 with 5km run time in age-group Hyrox athletes.
- If row pace drops >20 sec from training benchmark in race, it signals the preceding runs were too fast.

**Training Protocols**
- **Volume:** 3 × 500m @ race pace / 90 sec rest. Build to 5 × 500m.
- **Pace work:** 8 × 250m @ 5 sec/500m faster than race pace / 40 sec rest.
- **Race sim:** 1000m continuous → immediately into 400m run.
- Aim for 20,000–30,000m rowing per month in peak blocks.

### Station 6: Farmer's Carry 200m

**Target Times by Level**

| Level | Open Men (2×24kg) | Open Women (2×12kg) |
|---|---|---|
| Elite | sub-1:30 | sub-1:20 |
| Competitive | 1:30–2:15 | 1:20–2:00 |
| Recreational | 2:15–3:30 | 2:00–3:00 |

**Grip Training**
- This is where untrained athletes crack. After 5 stations and 5 runs, grip fails first.
- Grip-specific training: dead hangs (60+ sec sets), farmer's carry walks in training (4 × 50m), thick bar deadlifts, plate pinch holds.
- Chalk where available. Wrist straps not permitted in competition.
- Build grip endurance from week 1 of prep — it takes 8–10 weeks to develop meaningfully.

**Posture Cues**
- Shoulders back and down — do not allow them to roll forward under load.
- Neutral spine — no lateral lean or compensatory sway.
- Short, quick steps — do not overstride.
- Eyes forward, jaw relaxed.
- Breathe rhythmically — athletes who hold their breath lose coordination quickly.

**How to Break It Up**
- Most athletes attempt 200m straight and succeed in training. In race conditions, grip failure forces breaks.
- If you need to stop: grip reset takes <5 seconds. Don't put the weights down for longer.
- Strategic approach: aim for 100m unbroken, brief grip shake, 100m unbroken.
- Stronger athletes: 200m unbroken is achievable — eliminates all transition time cost.

### Station 7: Sandbag Lunges 100m

**Load by Category:** Open Women 10kg, Open Men 20kg, Pro Women 10kg, Pro Men 20kg (sandbag held to chest / bear hug)

**Target Times by Level**

| Level | Open Men (20kg) | Open Women (10kg) |
|---|---|---|
| Elite | sub-2:30 | sub-2:00 |
| Competitive | 2:30–3:30 | 2:00–3:00 |
| Recreational | 3:30–5:00 | 3:00–4:30 |

**Technique Cues**
- Sandbag held at chest height or in bear hug — not on shoulder (less spinal compression).
- Front knee tracks over the second toe on each lunge.
- Back knee gently touches or comes close to the floor — full depth required (judges watch).
- Upright torso — forward lean under load accelerates fatigue.
- Do not bounce out of the bottom — controlled descent, powerful drive.
- Step length: medium — overstride collapses the front knee; understride slows progress.

**Breathing Strategy**
- Exhale on the drive up, inhale on the descent.
- Do not hold breath for multiple reps — CO2 buildup causes dizziness under load.
- If lungs are burning from the preceding run, take 5–8 steady breaths at the start before launching into the lunges.

**Mental Strategies**
- Break into segments mentally: 25m / 25m / 25m / 25m.
- Count steps (not lunges) — gives more frequent micro-goals.
- Focus on the next 5 steps only — do not think about the full 100m when you're at metre 40.
- If technique breaks: brief stand and reset is faster than completing sloppy reps that get no-repped by judges.

**Training Volume**
- 4 × 25m lunges at race weight, 90 sec rest — build to continuous 100m.
- Weighted walking lunges in gym: 3 × 20 reps at 105–110% of sandbag weight.
- Add goblet squats + front-rack lunges for quad + core endurance.

### Station 8: Wall Balls

**Reps by Division:** Open Men/Women: 75 reps. Pro Men/Women: 100 reps.
**Ball Weight:** Open Women 4kg, Open Men 6kg. Pro same.
**Target Height:** 10 feet (standard).

**Target Times by Level**

| Level | Open (75 reps) | Pro (100 reps) |
|---|---|---|
| Elite | sub-3:00 | sub-4:00 |
| Competitive | 3:00–4:30 | 4:00–6:00 |
| Recreational | 4:30–7:00 | n/a |

**Technique Cues**
- Squat depth: thighs parallel to floor minimum — judges will no-rep shallow squats.
- Drive from the legs — the ball goes up with leg power, not arm push.
- Catch the ball at chin/chest height, absorb it into the squat — do not catch and stop.
- Eyes fixed on the target — not the ball.
- Keep elbows in and slightly forward — not flared wide.
- Breath: exhale sharply at the top of each throw. Inhale during descent.

**How to Pace the Set**
- First-timer strategy: sets of 15, rest 5 breaths, repeat. Do not attempt unbroken.
- Competitive strategy: 25–30 reps unbroken, brief rest, 20, brief rest, 15+. Or 15/15/15/15/15 with 3-breath breaks.
- Elite strategy: 75 reps unbroken or 50+25.
- Biggest mistake: going unbroken for 30–40 reps, then fully stopping for 20+ seconds. The fatigue cost of that break exceeds the time cost of planned shorter rests.
- **Mental anchor:** count down from the target rep count, not up. Counting down creates a closing sensation.

**Training Volume**
- 5 × 15 reps at race weight, 60 sec rest.
- Progress to 3 × 25, then 2 × 35, then 1 × 50 + 1 × 25.
- Timed sets: AMRAP 4 minutes at race weight — target 35–50 reps for competitive athletes.
- Race sim: 400m run → 75 wall balls for time. Repeat weekly in peak block.

## 3. Transition Strategy

### Run-to-Station Transitions

The transition is not passive — it is a deliberate physiological reset.

**What happens in the final 200m of each run:**
- Begin controlled breathing (nose in, mouth out) to prime recovery.
- Reduce run pace by 5–10% in the last 100m — this does not meaningfully add to run time but drops HR 8–12 BPM before station start.
- Scan the station setup as you approach — know where to stand, how to grip, where to face.

**Heart Rate Management**
- Target HR at station start: at or below 85% of HRmax.
- If HR is above 90% when arriving at a station, the station will hurt disproportionately.
- The run should be the recovery as well as the competition.
- Athletes who run too fast (see Section 4) arrive at stations pre-loaded with lactate. The station then becomes an interval repeat rather than a sustainable effort.

**Mental Reset Protocol**
- 3-second anchor: before starting a station, take one deliberate breath and set your intent. "Fast and smooth" or a target time cue.
- Do not start the station in panic mode — frenzied starts waste energy and cause technique breakdown in the first 10 seconds.
- After completing a station, take 3 walking steps before starting the run — do not sprint off the mat. These 3 seconds reduce HR spike and prevent the "charge and crash" pattern.

**What Elite Athletes Do Differently**
- They look calm. Their transitions are measured — not rushed.
- They walk the first 5m of each run to let the lungs adapt, then build to race pace over 50–100m.
- They know their split targets for every station and every run before they cross the start line.
- They use the final lap of each run as active recovery: controlled breathing, relaxed shoulders, mental prep for the incoming station.
- They do not chase other athletes. Race execution is internal, not reactive.

## 4. Race Pacing

### The Core Problem

The most common Hyrox mistake is running the first 2–3 laps 15–25% faster than sustainable race pace. This elevates blood lactate above threshold, which:
- Makes every subsequent station harder
- Causes run pace collapse in laps 5–8
- Creates the "death spiral" — slow stations → mental frustration → faster desperate running → more lactate

### Target Run Pacing

**Guideline: Hyrox 1km pace = 5km race pace + 30–50 seconds per km**

| 5km PB | Target Hyrox 1km pace |
|---|---|
| 17:00 (3:24/km) | 3:50–4:00/km |
| 20:00 (4:00/km) | 4:30–4:45/km |
| 22:00 (4:24/km) | 4:55–5:10/km |
| 25:00 (5:00/km) | 5:30–5:50/km |
| 28:00 (5:36/km) | 6:05–6:20/km |
| 32:00 (6:24/km) | 6:55–7:10/km |

**Why the gap from 5km pace?** Because you are running 8km of intervals with 20–40 minutes of station work accumulating fatigue throughout. The "effective" aerobic cost of Hyrox run km is 15–20% higher than a standalone run.

### How Run Pace Changes Across 8 Laps

A well-paced athlete shows:
- Laps 1–3: consistent pace at target
- Laps 4–5: 5–10 sec/km slower than target (sled fatigue, BBJ fatigue)
- Laps 6–7: return to target pace (second wind)
- Lap 8: 5–15 sec/km faster than target (final push)

A poorly paced athlete shows:
- Laps 1–2: 20–30 sec/km faster than sustainable
- Laps 3–4: at target (feels right but is already accumulated debt)
- Laps 5–7: 30–60 sec/km slower than target (paying the debt)
- Lap 8: slower than lap 5 (no reserve left)

### Negative Splitting vs Even Split

**Recommendation: even splits with a negative final lap.**

For most age-group athletes, aiming for even splits across all 8 laps is the safest strategy. The final 200m of lap 8 can be a sprint.

Pure negative split (getting progressively faster) is extremely difficult due to cumulative station fatigue — the body does not recover enough between stations to allow it.

**Practical pacing cues:**
- Lap 1 should feel embarrassingly easy. If it doesn't, you are going too fast.
- Use a GPS watch and set a pace alert. The crowd, music, and adrenaline of lap 1 will push you 20% too fast without feedback.
- Know your target 1km time and run by time, not feel.

## 5. Training Programming for Hyrox

### 12–16 Week Block Structure

#### Phase 1: Base Building (Weeks 1–4)
**Objective:** Aerobic base, movement quality, introduce stations at sub-maximal loads.

- Run volume: 35–50km/week (easy + moderate)
- Station exposure: 2 × per week, one station per session, focus on technique not speed
- Strength: 3 × per week, general compound movements (squat, hinge, push, pull)
- Long run: 8–12km easy (builds aerobic base for the 8km race distance)
- Rest/recovery: 1 full day + 1 active recovery

#### Phase 2: Specific Conditioning (Weeks 5–9)
**Objective:** Introduce combo workouts, race-pace intensity, station benchmarks.

- Run volume: 40–60km/week
- Combo workouts: 2–3 × per week (run immediately followed by station; see below)
- Station practice: benchmark testing at Weeks 5 and 9
- Strength: 2 × per week (maintain, not build)
- Long run: 10–14km, include race-pace km blocks
- Threshold run: 1 × per week (5–6km at race pace or faster)

#### Phase 3: Race Simulation + Peak (Weeks 10–13)
**Objective:** Full race simulation, peak fitness, confidence building.

- Full Hyrox simulation: 1 × in week 10 and 1 × in week 12
- Run volume: reduce by 10–15% from peak
- Station sessions: race weight, race distances
- Strength: 1–2 × per week, maintenance loads
- Key workout: 4 × 1km at target race pace / 90 sec rest — this is the primary run prep
- Race-specific combos: run → station → run (2-station combos)

#### Phase 4: Taper (Weeks 14–15 for 16-week block; Weeks 12–13 for 12-week)
**Objective:** Arrive fresh, sharp, and confident.

- Run volume: reduce by 35–40% from peak
- Station practice: race weight, 50–60% of normal volume, maintain speed
- Strength: 1 × only, 50–60% of normal volume
- No new training stimuli — this is maintenance, not loading
- Sleep and nutrition prioritized
- Final workout: 3 days before race — 20 min easy run + 2 × 15 wall balls at race weight. Nothing more.

### Weekly Training Structure (Peak Block Example)

| Day | Session |
|---|---|
| Monday | Combo workout: 3 × (1km run + SkiErg 500m + 1km run), 3 min rest between rounds |
| Tuesday | Strength: squat focus (back squat, Bulgarian split squat, hip thrust) |
| Wednesday | Threshold run: 5–6km at or near target Hyrox 1km pace |
| Thursday | Station day: Sled push + pull + farmer's carry + sandbag lunges (30–40 min) |
| Friday | Strength: pull focus (rows, deadlifts) + wall ball practice |
| Saturday | Long run 10–14km easy OR full simulation |
| Sunday | Complete rest or active recovery (30 min walk/swim) |

### Combo Workouts — The Most Important Training Tool

A combo workout places a run immediately before or after a station with no rest. This replicates the race-specific demand.

**Key Combos:**
1. **Run-SkiErg-Run:** 1km run → 500m SkiErg → 1km run. 3 rounds. Trains aerobic recovery.
2. **Run-Sled-Run:** 400m run → 20m sled push at race weight → 400m run. 4 rounds.
3. **Run-Row-Run:** 1km run → 500m row → 1km run. Mimics race feeling most closely.
4. **Run-Wall Ball-Run:** 800m run → 30 wall balls → 800m run. Essential for the final station.
5. **Full simulation (4-station):** 4 × (1km run + 1 station from sequence), in order.

**Why combos matter:** The heart rate does not return to normal between run and station in a race. The station is performed at 85–92% HRmax for most athletes. Combo workouts train the body to produce station output at elevated HR — which is the specific demand of Hyrox.

### Taper Protocol

- Reduce weekly volume by 35–40% in final 10–14 days.
- Maintain intensity — do not slow down or reduce weights. Reduce sets/reps, not load.
- Sleep increases to 8–9 hrs/night in taper week.
- Hydration focus: 500ml above normal daily intake in final 3 days.
- Last hard workout: 10–12 days before race.
- Race week: easy run Tuesday, light station work Thursday (15 min only), rest Friday.

### Key Benchmark Workouts

| Benchmark | Purpose | Timing |
|---|---|---|
| 1km time trial | Establish run baseline and target pace | Week 1, Week 8 |
| 5km time trial | Validate Hyrox run pace formula | Week 2, Week 10 |
| SkiErg 1000m for time | Station benchmark | Week 4, Week 9 |
| Row 1000m for time | Station benchmark | Week 4, Week 9 |
| Wall ball AMRAP 4 min | Capacity test | Week 4, Week 9 |
| Burpee broad jump 40m | Pacing + quality | Week 6 |
| Farmer's carry 200m unbroken | Grip endurance | Week 5, Week 10 |
| Sandbag lunge 100m for time | Full station simulation | Week 6, Week 11 |
| Full Hyrox simulation | Race rehearsal | Week 10, Week 12 |
| 4 × 1km at goal race pace | Run specificity | Weekly from Week 8 |

## 6. The Run Component

### Why Running Is the Race

Of a 75-minute Hyrox finish, approximately 45–50 minutes is spent running (8km at ~5:30–6:15/km pace). The stations account for 25–30 minutes. Therefore running fitness is the single largest lever on finish time.

**Minimum Run Fitness for Hyrox**
- Completing Hyrox under 90 minutes: 5km under 27:00
- Completing under 75 minutes: 5km under 22:00
- Competing at a high amateur level (sub-65 min): 5km under 19:30

### How to Improve Run Economy for Hyrox

Hyrox run economy is different from standalone running because:
1. Runs are short (1km) — acceleration and settling into pace are proportionally more important.
2. Each run begins after a fatiguing station — the neuromuscular state is compromised.
3. Breathing is already elevated when the run begins.

**Training interventions:**
- **Repeated 1km intervals** at and slightly above target race pace: 6–10 × 1km / 90 sec rest. The single best Hyrox-specific run workout.
- **Short tempo runs:** 4 × 1.5km at threshold. Trains lactate clearance during the runs.
- **Strides:** 6–8 × 80m strides after easy runs. Maintains fast-twitch recruitment and stride mechanics.
- **Running form:** cadence 170–180 spm. Do not overstride. Forward lean from the ankles. Arms driving backward (not crossing body). Hyrox athletes tend to lose form under fatigue — film yourself in training to identify collapse.

### Brick-Style Training

A "brick" in triathlon = bike immediately followed by run. For Hyrox: station immediately followed by run.

**Why this is essential:** The legs feel completely different starting a run immediately after 3 minutes of sled pushing. The muscle recruitment pattern is disrupted. Brick training teaches the body to re-engage running mechanics quickly.

**Recommended Bricks:**
- SkiErg 500m → 1km run × 4
- Row 1000m → 1km run × 3
- Sandbag lunges 50m → 800m run × 4
- Wall balls 30 reps → 1km run × 3
- Full-station → 1km run: progress to performing this in race sequence

### Managing Cumulative Run Fatigue

By runs 6, 7, and 8, athletes have performed 5–7 stations and 5–7 × 1km. Neuromuscular fatigue is significant.

**Training strategies:**
- Long run with race-pace inserts: 10km run with km 4, 6, 8, and 10 at target race pace. Simulates late-race running on tired legs.
- Station-heavy day + run: complete 3 stations back-to-back, then immediately perform a 2km run at race pace.
- Progressive 8 × 1km: run 8 × 1km at target pace with only 60 sec recovery. The later intervals simulate race lap 6–8 conditions.

## 7. Strength Requirements

### Minimum Strength Standards

These are the strength levels at which the Hyrox loads feel manageable (not easy, but not the limiting factor in the race):

| Movement | Open Men Standard | Open Women Standard |
|---|---|---|
| Back squat | 100kg / 1.2× BW | 60kg / 1.0× BW |
| Deadlift | 120kg / 1.5× BW | 70kg / 1.1× BW |
| Farmer's carry | 2×28kg / 200m | 2×16kg / 200m |
| Sandbag carry lunge | 24kg / 100m | 12kg / 100m |
| Wall balls unbroken | 30 reps | 30 reps |
| Strict pull-up | 5 reps | 1–3 reps |

Athletes below these standards should spend proportionally more time on strength work in weeks 1–8 of prep.

### Gym Exercises That Transfer Most

| Station | Primary Transfer Exercises |
|---|---|
| SkiErg | Lat pull-down, cable rows, banded pull-aparts, pallof press |
| Sled Push | Back squat, hip thrust, Bulgarian split squat, box step-up |
| Sled Pull | Bent-over row, Pendlay row, cable row, deadlift |
| Burpee Broad Jump | Broad jump practice, box jump, burpee volume |
| Rowing | Deadlift, power clean, barbell row, core work |
| Farmer's Carry | Hex bar deadlift, dumbbell farmer's carry, dead hangs |
| Sandbag Lunges | Goblet lunge, front rack lunge, zercher squat |
| Wall Balls | Front squat, goblet squat, medicine ball throws |

### Balancing Strength vs Endurance

**Golden ratio for Hyrox prep:** 60% aerobic/cardio training, 40% strength/station work in a balanced week.

For an athlete with a strong strength background but weak running: shift to 70% aerobic, 30% strength.
For an athlete with a strong running background but weak stations: shift to 50% aerobic, 50% strength.

**Critical principle:** Strength in Hyrox is about endurance of moderate loads, not maximal strength. A 160kg back squat does not mean you can push a sled faster than someone who squats 120kg but has trained combo workouts for 12 weeks.

### Weight Room Priorities Per Block Phase

- **Weeks 1–4:** Build strength. 4–5 sets of 4–6 reps. Heavy compounds. Station loads light.
- **Weeks 5–9:** Shift to strength endurance. 3–4 sets of 10–15 reps. Station loads at race weight.
- **Weeks 10–13:** Maintain. 2–3 sets of 8–12 reps. Priority is combo workouts not isolated lifts.
- **Taper:** 1–2 sessions, moderate weight, low fatigue. No new loading.

## 8. Nutrition & Recovery

### Pre-Race Fuelling

**3 Days Before (Carbohydrate Loading)**
- Increase carbohydrate intake to 7–9g/kg bodyweight/day.
- Sources: rice, pasta, oats, sweet potato, bananas.
- Keep fat and fibre moderate — do not overload the gut.
- Hydration: 2.5–3.5L water/day + electrolytes.

**Race Morning (typically a 10:00–12:00 start)**
- Wake 3–3.5 hours before start.
- Meal 2.5–3 hours before: 1–1.5g/kg carbs, low fat, low fibre. Example: 2 slices toast + jam + 1 banana + coffee.
- 60–75 min before: 30–40g fast carbs (banana, energy gel, sports drink).
- 30 min before: 200ml electrolyte drink. No solid food.
- Caffeine: 3–6mg/kg bodyweight 45–60 min before start (coffee, caffeine gel). Proven 3–5% performance benefit.

**In-Race Fuelling**
- Hyrox typically takes 55–110 minutes — within the glycogen window for most athletes (depletion occurs ~90 min+).
- For athletes targeting sub-70 minutes: no in-race fuelling needed if pre-loaded correctly.
- For athletes over 75–80 minutes: consider 1 gel (20–25g carbs) between station 4 and station 5 run (pocket or hand carry).
- Hydration: drink stations on the run laps — sip if possible, do not over-drink. 150–200ml per lap if stations are available.

**What NOT to do on race morning:**
- Do not try new foods on race morning.
- Do not skip breakfast (glycogen stores are partially depleted overnight).
- Do not take ibuprofen or aspirin pre-race (gut issues + reduces inflammatory response needed for performance).

### Recovery from a Hyrox Race

Hyrox is extremely demanding — muscle damage comparable to a half marathon + a heavy strength session simultaneously.

**Day of race (post-finish):**
- Within 30 minutes: 25–30g protein + 60–80g fast carbs. Chocolate milk, recovery shake + banana, or similar.
- Hydrate to replace: weigh yourself pre and post; replace 150% of weight lost in fluid.
- Salt: electrolyte tabs or salty snack to replace sodium lost in sweat.

**Days 1–3 post-race:**
- Expect significant DOMS in glutes, quads, hamstrings, lats, and grip.
- Active recovery only: walk, swim, gentle cycle.
- Sleep: target 9 hrs/night.
- Protein: maintain 1.8–2.2g/kg daily to support muscle repair.
- Do not run or do station work.

**Days 4–7 post-race:**
- Begin easy movement if DOMS allows.
- Short easy run (20–25 min) if legs feel ready on day 5–6.
- No intensity for 7 days.

**Full return to training:** 10–14 days post-race for easy sessions; 3 weeks before any intensity work.

### Fuelling Heavy Training Weeks

During peak training (weeks 8–12 of prep):
- Total daily calories: 400–700kcal above baseline.
- Carbohydrates: 5–8g/kg/day on high volume days; 4–5g/kg on rest/easy days.
- Protein: 1.8–2.2g/kg daily (non-negotiable for muscle adaptation and recovery).
- Fat: 1.0–1.2g/kg (supports hormonal health and fat metabolism).
- Pre-session (45–60 min before): 30–50g carbs if session >60 min.
- Post-session (within 45 min): 25–35g protein + 50–70g carbs.

## 9. Common Hyrox Athlete Mistakes

### By Station

**SkiErg**
- Mistake: Arms-only pull, no hip hinge. Fix: Practise the hip hinge pattern without the erg first (Romanian deadlift motion), then apply to the machine.
- Mistake: Pace too fast in first 200m. Fix: Set a target 500m split, programme damper to read pace, hold it from stroke 1.

**Sled Push**
- Mistake: Hips too high, not enough lean. Fix: Coach or film from the side during training.
- Mistake: Stopping mid-push and restarting — loses all momentum. Fix: Short steps, keep pressure constant.

**Sled Pull**
- Mistake: Pulling with arms only, no body sway. Fix: Sit into a hip hinge on each pull, use body weight.
- Mistake: Letting rope go slack between pulls. Fix: Maintain slight tension at all times.

**Burpee Broad Jump**
- Mistake: Half-heartedly jumping for distance — taking 25+ jumps to cover 80m. Fix: Film a 20-jump set and measure total distance. Hold yourself to 1.5m minimum per jump in training.
- Mistake: Incomplete chest-to-floor contact. Fix: Programme strict Hyrox-standard burpees from day 1 of training — no half reps.

**Rowing**
- Mistake: Not using legs — "arm row" pattern under fatigue. Fix: Drill "legs-only" rowing (arms fixed at body, drive with legs only) for 10 strokes to re-establish pattern.
- Mistake: Going out too fast in first 200m. Fix: Set a target split on the monitor and stick to it.

**Farmer's Carry**
- Mistake: Grip failure causes multiple drops. Fix: Begin grip training in week 1. Dead hangs 3 × per week minimum.
- Mistake: Leaning to one side or bending forward. Fix: Practise carries with eyes on a point ahead, posture cued by partner or mirror.

**Sandbag Lunges**
- Mistake: Forward torso lean causes knee pain + slower movement. Fix: Practise lunges with sandbag against a wall to force upright posture.
- Mistake: Inconsistent depth (some reps partial). Fix: Use a rubber mat on the floor — back knee must visibly lower.
- Mistake: No-reps from judge for depth. Fix: Train race-standard depth from day 1. Never train partial reps.

**Wall Balls**
- Mistake: Catching too low (below chin), losing energy return. Fix: Hands always at chin height as baseline catch position.
- Mistake: Going out at a pace that requires stopping for 15–20+ seconds mid-set. Fix: Practise the "50+25" or "25/25/15/10" strategy, building the specific capacity to push through rep 40–50 discomfort.
- Mistake: Shallow squat = no-rep. Fix: Film side-on in training. Thighs must reach parallel.

### Race Strategy Mistakes

- **Starting too fast on the first run.** The most common and most damaging mistake. There is no way to "bank time" in Hyrox — excess early effort compounds into slower later performance.
- **Not eating enough before the race.** Athletes who skip breakfast or eat <2 hours before start frequently "hit the wall" at lap 6–7.
- **Ignoring the carry and lunge stations in training.** Most athletes train SkiErg and row but avoid carries and lunges. The carries and lunges punish the undertrained athlete severely in race conditions.
- **No race-day warm-up.** Minimum 12–15 minutes of movement — easy jog, hip openers, dynamic leg swings, 10 wall balls, 3 sled pushes at low weight. Without warm-up, lap 1 is hard and station 1 is dangerous.
- **Wearing the wrong shoes.** Cushioned running shoes are acceptable but not optimal. Hyrox shoes (low heel drop, grippy sole) improve sled performance and burpee comfort. Train in race-day shoes for at least 8 weeks.

### How Coaches Spot These Errors

- Watch for HR spike and pace crash in laps 3–5 (overcooked early laps).
- Check training logs for missing carry and lunge sessions.
- Film station technique — most athletes have never seen themselves on the SkiErg or under a sandbag.
- Ask about training shoes vs race shoes.
- Review simulation times: if simulation time is 10+ minutes off goal, the goal is unrealistic and pace planning must be adjusted.

## 10. Doubles Strategy

### Division of Work

In Hyrox Doubles (pairs), each athlete performs half the total distance and reps at each station. However, the strategy for division is not simply "take turns equally."

**Recommended work division principles:**
- Play to individual strengths: if athlete A is a strong rower and athlete B is a strong skier, let A lead the rowing station.
- Manage fatigue: the stronger runner should take longer turns on stations that immediately precede the hardest stations for the other athlete.
- Never let the transition between athletes exceed 5 seconds at any station — handoffs must be practised.

### Communication Protocols

**Before the race:**
- Agree on a clear signal for "I'm coming off" — tap the shoulder, verbal "go", or eye contact. Ambiguity costs time.
- Agree on the run pacing — one partner should not pull ahead on the laps.
- Set a "no-rescue" rule for the first half: do not let one athlete do most of the work before the halfway point out of anxiety.

**During the race:**
- Short verbal cues only: "Pace", "Change", "Good", "More". Long sentences during exercise are inefficient.
- On the row and SkiErg: call out every 250m split so both athletes are informed.
- On carries: one athlete can spot/guide while the other works, then switch.

**Transition Protocols**
- Sled push/pull: clear the handle/rope completely before the next athlete grabs it.
- Row and SkiErg: the incoming athlete places hands on before the outgoing athlete releases.
- Carry: set down fully before partner picks up — dragging weight from a partner's hands causes injuries.
- Wall balls and lunges: face to face swap, ball handoff is a single-count movement.

### Doubles-Specific Training

- Train all handoffs in every combo workout from week 6 onwards.
- Practice the changeover at fatigue, not just fresh.
- Run together in all training runs — race together or learn to match pace.
- Simulate the "you go, I recover" pattern: one athlete works intensely for 90 seconds while the other actively recovers — this is what the doubles stations feel like.
